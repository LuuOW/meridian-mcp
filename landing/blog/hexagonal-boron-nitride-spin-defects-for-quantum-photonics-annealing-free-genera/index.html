Hexagonal Boron Nitride Spin Defects for Quantum Photonics: Annealing-Free Generation by Krypton Ion Implantation

Kr+ ion implantation produces luminescent spin defects in hexagonal boron nitride at room temperature with no anneal step, giving a chemically inert, single-recipe route to NIR quantum-photonics emitters.

Paper: arXiv:2606.23334
Schematic: 40 keV Kr+ ions deposit a thin VN–CB defect layer near the surface of an hBN flake. No pre- or post-implant anneal is required for the PL band to appear.

arXiv:2606.23334 is a fabrication paper with a sharp practical claim: Shyam, Singh, Akkanaboina et al. generate room-temperature near-infrared luminescent spin defects in hexagonal boron nitride by krypton-ion implantation, with no pre- or post-implantation anneal. The defect layer is stable, the recipe is a single step at room temperature, and the chemical inertness of Kr+ in the hBN lattice avoids the surface chemistry that has plagued earlier recipes.

That last point is what separates this result from the rest of the hBN-defect literature. Until now, every published route to a luminescent hBN defect has needed a thermal anneal — either to activate the implant, to heal surface damage, or to migrate the defect into a stable configuration. The anneal is what makes reproducibility hard, because anneal temperature and ambient atmosphere dominate the variance. Removing it collapses the process to a single ion-implant step.

Why an annealing-free route matters

Hexagonal boron nitride has been the third solid-state quantum emitter host for about six years, sitting alongside diamond (NV, SiV) and silicon carbide (divacancy, VSi). Its attraction is a wide bandgap (~6 eV), a low natural-abundance of nuclear spins, and a Debye–Waller factor that pushes the phonon sideband well below the zero-phonon line for several defect species. The blocker has always been reproducibility: defect yield is sensitive to implantation dose, anneal time, anneal temperature, ambient chemistry, and substrate quality, and no two published recipes agree on the right combination.

The new paper removes the anneal axis entirely. That collapses the parameter space by one dimension and makes the process friendly to standard ion-implant lines that don't carry a high-temperature anneal module.

What the team measured

The authors ran SRIM Monte Carlo simulations to optimise the implant parameters, then irradiated hBN flakes with 40 keV Kr+ at fluences from 1011 to 1015 ions/cm2. They report three measurements that pin down the defect.

First, a stable near-infrared photoluminescence band centred at ~830 nm, whose intensity scales monotonically with fluence over three decades. Second, temperature-dependent PL linewidth (20–300 K) that fits a clean T3 dependence:

\[ \Gamma(T) = \Gamma_0 + \alpha\, T^3, \qquad \alpha \sim 10^{-7}\,\mathrm{nm/K^3}. \]

The T3 scaling is the textbook signature of acoustic-phonon-mediated dephasing via two-phonon Raman scattering. It's the right physical mechanism, and it gives a single number (\(\alpha\)) that lets a process engineer compare recipes across labs.

Third, Raman spectroscopy confirms the implant writes disorder into the lattice. The pristine hBN E2g mode at ~1366 cm−1 survives, and a new defect-induced feature appears at ~1295 cm−1, consistent with bond breaking around the implanted ions.

Finally, electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) shows a paramagnetic centre with g = 2.003. DFT calculations attribute that to a spatially separated VN–CB donor-acceptor pair — the substitutional nitrogen vacancy paired with a carbon-on-boron antisite a few lattice sites away. That separation is what makes the centre addressable as a single defect: the two charges don't collapse onto one site.

Why g = 2.003 matters: free-electron g is 2.0023. A defect with g − ge ≈ 0.0007 is a clean spin-1/2 with weak spin-orbit coupling. That is exactly what you want for an optically addressable qubit at room temperature — the spin relaxation stays long, and the optical contrast stays readable.

What the recipe buys

The single-step, room-temperature recipe has three practical consequences.

  • Standard ion-implant lines work. No furnace, no controlled atmosphere, no anneal chamber. A fab with a standard 40 keV implant tool can do this run.
  • Yield is dose-driven, not chemistry-driven. The PL intensity scales with fluence over 1011–1015 ions/cm2. That is the dimension you want to be the dominant variable.
  • The defect identity is fixed by the SRIM profile. The 40 keV implant deposits energy within a ~30 nm surface layer. The defect is shallow, spatially localised, and reproducible.
The point of the paper is not the defect — it is the recipe. Removing the anneal axis makes hBN defect generation compatible with the same fab flow that already produces diamond NV centres. That is the manufacturing story.

What is open next

Three things the paper does not yet answer, and that a process engineer would want before committing to a roadmap:

  • Single-defect addressability. The optical measurements are ensemble. Site-controlled single defects are the next milestone — typically via pre-patterned apertures in a PMMA mask, or via focused ion beam (FIB) implantation.
  • Spin coherence at room temperature. T2 and T1 measurements on the ensemble, and ultimately on single defects, are what determines whether the centre is competitive with NV diamond for room-temperature quantum sensing.
  • Defect stability under device processing. Does the luminescent band survive typical back-end-of-line steps — dielectric deposition, anneals at 400 °C, metal lift-off? For most solid-state emitters this is where the real-world failure mode lives.

What I'd keep in mind

Three numbers worth carrying forward from this paper, the same way I would from any solid-state-emitter fabrication result.

  • Process window: 40 keV Kr+, 1011–1015 ions/cm2, no anneal. Anything outside that fluence range is unverified.
  • PL signature: ~830 nm zero-phonon, T3 broadening, g = 2.003. The T3 coefficient is the most useful single number for cross-lab comparison.
  • Defect identity: spatially separated VN–CB donor-acceptor pair, per DFT. Treat this as a hypothesis until single-defect data confirms it.

What I would not over-claim: the ensemble measurements tell you the defect exists and is optically active. They do not tell you it is a useful qubit yet. Single-defect addressability and spin coherence are the gates between “recipe works” and “qubit”.

Takeaway

Kr+ implantation on hBN produces a stable room-temperature NIR spin defect with no anneal step. The recipe is a single ion-implant step at room temperature, using a standard 40 keV tool, with a chemically inert species. That collapses the hBN defect-generation process space by one dimension and makes the route compatible with the same fab flow that already produces diamond NV centres.

That is the standard to hold the next hBN-defect paper to: ensemble PL is not enough. Show single-defect addressability, T1/T2 at the single-emitter level, and survival through back-end-of-line processing. The recipe is now good enough that those questions are the right ones to ask.
